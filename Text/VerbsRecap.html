Verbs recap

Analyzing and looking up verb forms


Let's start with these two verb forms:

  • τεῦχε (from the sentence αὐτοὺς δὲ ἑλώρια τεῦχε κύνεσσιν / οἰωνοῖσί τε δαῖτα)
  • καθεῦδε (from the sentence ὣς ὁ μὲν ἔνθα καθεῦδε)

So -ε is the ending of the 3rd pers sg act, for past tenses of -ω Verbs. For -μι verbs, there is no distinct ending, -ε corresponding to the thematic vowel.
So from the -ε ending we know it's a 3^ sg in an active past tense: aorist, imperfect, pluperfect in the indicative, or in the optative (all the optative tenses use the secondary past endings, for present, furure, aorist, perfect).
We could already suppose both τεῦχε and καθεῦδε might be imperferct tenses because there is not –σ– a the end of the stem, which is found in some aorist and future forms, and it does not have the reduplication of the perfect, or of some older forms of the aorist. Also we can rule out the optative because both τεῦχε and καθεῦδε lack the characteristic –ι– or –ιη- optative suffix between the stem and the endings.
Looking up in the dictionary (Montanari's one, for example), we find the entry τεύχω (present ind act 1sg), for which the imperfect is listed as ἔτευχον (and in the epic form without augment: τεῦχον), and the aorist as ἔτευξα. From this we conclude that the form τεῦχε is using the present stem, with a past ending, so it must be in the imperfect tense. So it's an imperfect indicative active in the 3rd pers. sg, in an epic-homeric form withtou augment, with meaning "it made" (the subject is Achilles' wrath, which made the corpses of the heroes into prey for dogs and feast for crows).
For καθεῦδε, the same reasoning applies, it most likely seems to be an imperfect (no –σ– and no reduplication). In the dictionary we find καθεύδω (ion. κατεύδω), with impf. καθεῦδον (o καθῆυδον) / ἐκάθευδον. The dictionary does not list the other paradigm forms. It derives from κατά + εὕδω. εὕδω (impf. ηὗδον, aor. ηὕδησα) "to sleep", is generally used in composites. Other forms: impf. εὗδον, iter. εὕδεσκον ‖ att. gener. καθεύδω. So we conclude that καθεῦδε is indeed a form of the imperfect active in the 3rd pers sg, meaning "he was sleeping down".


Let's see two more verb forms in the imperfect tense, but in the middle or passive voice:

  • ἐτελείετο (from the sentence Διὸς δ᾽ ἐτελείετο βουλή)
  • ἔφατο (from the sentence ὣς ἔφατ᾽)

From the endings table, we see that –το is the ending for the 3rd pers sg middle or passive for past tenses. So this again might be either an imperfect, aorist, or pluperfet.
We see that both start with the augment –ἐ, so for ἐτελείετο we look up in the dictionary for a present starting with τελε–. For the verb τελέω, we find that in homeric Greek has the form ἐτελείετο for the 3^ pers. sg middle-passive voice. So ἐτελείετο means "it was being fulfilled".

For ἔφατο the search might not be so straightforward because the present stem seems not derivable from this form (by removing the augment –ἐ, and the personal ending –το, we would look for a verb starting with φα– in the present). But this is one of the most frequently used verbs (φημί, "to say", in position n. 41 in Dickinson College's Greek Core Vocabulary), with a limited paradigm and an imperfect middle 1st pers sg in ἐφάμην.


Let's see three more verb forms:

  • ἔγροιτο (from the sentence ὡς Ὀδυσεὺς ἔγροιτο, ἴδοι τ᾽ ἐυώπιδα κούρην)
  • ἔθηκεν (from the sentence ἣ μυρί᾽ Ἀχαιοῖς ἄλγε᾽ ἔθηκεν)
  • προΐαψεν (from the sentence Ἄϊδι προΐαψεν)


ἔγροιτο is a 3rd pers sg middle-passive, in a past tense, or optative. The –ι suffix indicates it's an optative.
The present form ( ἐγείρω , "to wake up"), is not straightforward to find in the dictionary with the rules seen so far here. The initial ἔ– is not an augment, and the vowel change in the stem, from –γείρ– to –γρ– does not follow the typical apophony rules (Smyth 1920, § 27-59), according to which the additional –ι– is preserved at the zero degree in the aorist.
According to Chantraine (1968, p.310), the initial ἔ– might be either a prothetic vowel (Smyth 1920, § 41), or the remains of an aorist with reduplication (Smyth 1920, §§ 439-448, AGE ch. 6 and 42). In any case, the present ἐγείρω has been created from the aorist ἔγρετο.


ἔθηκεν is a 3rd pers sg active of a past tense. This is a very common verb, τίθημι ("to put, place; establish; put in a certain state; cause"), n. 164 in Dickinson College's Greek Core Vocabulary. The present has reduplication. The aorist 1sg active is ἔθηκα (this is one of those aorists formed with –κα instead of –σα, see AGE ch. 32). So the meaning of ἔθηκεν is "it caused".


προΐαψεν is again a 3rd pers sg active of a past tense. It seems to be a sigmatic aorist because the stem ends with –πσ–. Looking for προΐα– in the dictionary we find προϊάπτω ("to throw in front"), with aorist προΐαψα. So the meaning is "it threw".


Let's see two more verb forms:

  • ἀντέγραψαν (from the sentence ἀντέγραψαν ‘αἴκα.’)
  • γράψαντος (from the sentence γράψαντος αὐτοῖς τοῦ Φιλίππου)


ἀντέγραψαν ends with –σαν, so it must be a 3rd pers pl active, of a past tense of a –μι verb.
We can recognize that it is a composite of the prefix ἀντ– (from ἀντί ) + έγραψαν. έγραψαν seems an augmented form, so if we loot for ἀντίγρα– (withouth the augment), we find the verb ἀντιγράφω ("to write back"), derivative of γράφω, which has aorist έγραψα (so ἀντέγραψαν is using the supposed typical endings of –μι verbs, even though it is a –ω verb).
So ἀντέγραψαν means "they wrote back".


γράψαντος has the –ντ suffix, so it is a participle active.
We have to determine if γράψα– is a stem of the present, future or aorist (participles use the stems of the indicative mood, cf. Ancient Greek for Everyone, ch. 37), and to which gender, number and case corresponds the –ος ending.
γράψα– is most likely the base of an aorist active participle, because it ends with –σα (while the future would end with just –σ and the present with just the present stem, cf. Ancient Greek for Everyone, ch. 38).
The –ος ending is that of the genitive singular, masculine or neuter (cf Smyth 1920, §§ 305-310).
Therefore γράψαντος is an aorist particle active in the genitive singular. Since in the sentence it is paired with a noun also in the genitive (τοῦ Φιλίππου), it is a genitive absolute (cf. Ancient Greek for Everyone, ch. 39), meaning "when Philip wrote".
